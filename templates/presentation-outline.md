# Presentation Outline Template

For a conference talk / slide deck outline — structured for a live audience with a fixed time slot, not a reader who can re-read a paragraph. See `references/OUTPUT-TEMPLATES-GUIDE.md` for fit, `references/AUDIENCE-CALIBRATION.md` before drafting — this template needs it most, since a professional/peer audience (e.g. a UXR conference) changes the whole opening strategy vs a general one.

```md
# {Talk title}

**Audience**: {from Create Mode calibration — who they are, what they
already know coming in, what they're hoping to leave with}
**Target length**: {e.g. 20min talk + 5min Q&A}
**Branches drawn on**: {TOPIC-GRAPH.json branches/nodes this pulls from}

## Slide 1 — Hook / framing
{The opening claim or question that earns the room's attention in the
first 60 seconds. For a peer audience, this is often a reframe of
something they think they already understand, not a basic-concepts
intro. Speaker notes: what to say, not just what's on the slide.}

## Slide 2-3 — Why this matters to *this* audience
{Not a general motivation — connect explicitly to what a UXR/domain
audience cares about (their own practice, a problem they've hit).
This section doesn't exist in the essay/blog templates; a live
audience needs to be told why they should keep listening before
the substance arrives.}

## Slides {N} — {One cluster per major branch or theme}
{Each cluster = one graph branch or tightly related set of highlights.
For each: the concept, the concrete example/analogy (visual if possible —
reuse or regenerate ./images/ assets sized for a slide), and the
takeaway line to say out loud. Keep slide text minimal — the speaker
notes carry the depth, not the slide itself.}

## Slide {N+1} — Synthesis
{Where branches connect — same principle as the long-video template's
Act 3. This is usually where a peer audience's own "oh, that's why"
moment lands, if the talk is working.}

## Closing slide — Takeaway + call to action
{One line the audience should be able to repeat afterward. For a
professional audience, often ends in something applicable to their
own work, not just a summary of what was said.}

## Anticipated questions
{Given who's in the room, what will they push back on or ask about?
Draft brief answers — this doubles as Q&A prep.}
```

## Rules

- Slide count and pacing should map to actual time available — don't pad or overstuff; a live audience can't re-read a dense slide the way a reader can re-read a paragraph.
- Speaker notes are not optional — the slide is what's seen, the notes are what's said, and they're not the same text.
- Audience calibration determines slide 1's whole strategy: a domain-peer audience wants a reframe or a provocative claim, not a definitions-first intro.
