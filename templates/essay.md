# Essay Template (Medium-style)

For a personal, voice-driven long-form piece — more essay than blog-post-with-sections. See `references/OUTPUT-TEMPLATES-GUIDE.md` for fit, `references/AUDIENCE-CALIBRATION.md` before drafting.

```md
# {Title}

*{Audience: who this is for, what they already know — carried from the
Create Mode calibration step, not shown to the reader, just kept as a
header note for revision later}*

## Opening
{Start from what actually pulled the explorer in — the real question or
tension that kicked off this exploration. Essays earn the reader's trust
by being honest about the starting confusion, not by opening with a
polished thesis. "I thought it was X, until..." works here more than it does in a
blog post, because essay voice tolerates — even wants — first person.}

## The unfolding
{Unlike the blog template's per-section-per-highlight structure, an
essay can follow the actual shape of the thinking: false starts,
the branch that turned out to matter, the moment a connection clicked.
Let the graph's actual question-spawned branches show up as narrative
turns ("then I wondered whether—") rather than being smoothed into a
tidy outline. This is where the "thinking fingerprint" the whole
product is built around should be most visible.}

## The turn
{Where the exploration's strongest highlight — usually a reframe or
a connection across branches — lands. This is the essay's real payload;
everything before it is earning the reader's patience for this moment.}

## Closing
{Essays can end open — pointing at what's still unresolved, or what it
means for how the writer (and by extension the reader) now sees the
topic. Doesn't need the tidy synthesis a blog post's closing wants.}
```

## Rules

- More voice, less structure than the blog template — resist over-organizing into rigid sections if the actual exploration's shape was more meandering. That meander, if genuine, is the essay's value.
- Audience calibration matters more here than in any other template: an essay's whole appeal is a specific voice talking to a specific kind of reader. Don't write a generic "smart explainer" — write to the audience established in the calibration step.
- Still cite sources for factual claims (footnote-style or inline links), even though the voice is personal — accuracy isn't optional just because the tone loosened up.
